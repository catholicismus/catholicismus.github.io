Liège, 1374

← Back to Miracles

The 1374 Eucharistic Miracle of Liège (historically intertwined with the events of Middleburg and Louvain) is a recognized phenomenon in Catholic history that directly reinforced the Feast of Corpus Christi and the Catholic doctrine of transubstantiation.

The Core Event

The miracle originated in Middleburg, Belgium, but its verification, promotion, and liturgical impact became deeply connected to the religious authorities in Liège:

  • The Incident: A noblewoman's manservant named Jan, who had been living a dissolute life without going to confession for years, approached the altar to receive Holy Communion.
  • The Transformation: The moment the priest placed the Host on the man's tongue, the consecrated bread instantly changed into bleeding flesh.
  • The Reaction: Jan panicked, and part of the bleeding Host fell from his mouth. The priest carefully retrieved the miraculous particle and safely secured it inside the tabernacle.
  • The Conversion: Overcome by terror and remorse, Jan immediately confessed his sins and completely reformed his lifestyle.

Recognition and Relics

Ecclesiastical Approval: Civil and church authorities were quickly notified. After a diligent review, the local Archbishop formally authorized the public cult and devotion surrounding the miracle.

The Relics: The miraculous host was split into two parts to spread the devotion. One portion remained in Middleburg, while the other section was taken to Louvain (Leuven), where it was preserved by the Augustinian Fathers. Much of the official medieval documentation surrounding the miracle's authenticity is still archived in Belgium.

Liturgical Impact

While St. Juliana of Liège had originally received visions in the 13th century that led Pope Urban IV to institute the Feast of Corpus Christi, the 1374 miracle served as a massive local confirmation. It drastically revitalized public adoration of the Blessed Sacrament across Belgium, making Liège a central hub for Eucharistic devotion throughout Europe.
