Amsterdam, 1345

← Back to Miracles

The 1345 Eucharistic Miracle of Amsterdam occurred when a dying man vomited a consecrated Host. It was thrown into a fire but remained unburnt and radiant. This recognized event transformed medieval Amsterdam into a thriving pilgrimage hub, shaping the city's early development and inspiring modern commemorative traditions.

The Miracle Event

On March 15, 1345, a gravely ill fisherman named Ysbrand Dommer requested the Last Rites at his home on Kalverstraat. After receiving the Holy Eucharist, he suffered a violent coughing fit and vomited the Host. Following the instructions of his priest, his caretaker threw the vomit, along with the Host, into the burning fireplace. The next morning, the Host was discovered resting undamaged and glowing atop the ashes.

Investigation and Approval

The local parish priest retrieved the Host and took it to the Oude Kerk (Old Church), but it miraculously returned to the house on Kalverstraat twice. Local church authorities and the city council investigated the events and officially recognized them as a miracle.

Impact and Legacy

  • Heilige Stede: A major chapel, the Kapel ter Heilige Stede (Chapel of the Holy City), was erected on the site of the fireplace, turning Amsterdam into a major pilgrimage destination.
  • The Reformation: After the Protestant Reformation, the chapel was seized in 1578, and the Host was eventually lost.
  • Stille Omgang: Today, the event is commemorated annually by thousands of Dutch Catholics through the Stille Omgang (Silent Walk), a nighttime silent procession retracing the medieval route.
