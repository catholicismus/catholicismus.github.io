Weingarten, 1094

The Weingarten Eucharistic Miracle—more accurately known as the Miracle of the Holy Blood—revolves around a highly venerated relic of the Most Precious Blood of Jesus Christ. Located in the Benedictine Abbey of St. Martin in Weingarten, Germany, the relic's origins are traditionally traced back to the Crucifixion itself.

History of the Relic

  • Tradition: According to medieval tradition, the Roman soldier Longinus caught some of the blood and water flowing from the side of Jesus and preserved it in a lead box.
  • Discovery: The box was supposedly hidden for centuries in Mantua, Italy, until it was miraculously discovered in 804.
  • Division: In 1048, the relic was officially recognized by Pope Leo IX and Emperor Henry III and divided into three parts.
  • Arrival in Weingarten: The relic eventually made its way to Weingarten in 1094 through Countess Judith of Flanders, who gifted the precious blood to the abbey.

The Annual Procession ("Blutritt")

The Weingarten relic is incredibly famous for its massive annual equestrian procession, known as the Blutritt (Ride of the Blood). Held on the Friday after Ascension Day, this tradition features up to 3,000 horsemen and thousands of pilgrims. It is one of the largest equestrian processions in Europe, serving as a powerful public witness of faith.
