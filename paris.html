Paris, 1290

← Back to Miracles

The 1290 Eucharistic Miracle of Paris is a highly venerated event in Catholic tradition, centered around the alleged desecration of a consecrated Host by a Jewish moneylender. According to the historical account, the host miraculously resisted multiple attempts to destroy it, bleeding profusely and transforming into a crucifix.

The Narrative of the Miracle

  • The Desecration: During Easter of 1290, a man fiercely hostile to the Christian faith obtained a consecrated Host. He reportedly stabbed it with a knife, causing it to bleed.
  • Failed Destruction: In a panic, the man attempted to burn the Host, but it miraculously hovered out of the flames. He then threw it into boiling water, but the Host rose from the water, hovered in mid-air, and took the form of a bleeding crucifix.
  • The Recovery: The terrified man eventually placed the Host into a basin and gave it to a pious woman, who safely delivered it to her parish priest at the church of Saint-Jean-en-Grève.

Historical Consequences

Execution and Confiscation: Following the accusation, the man was condemned and burned at the stake. King Philip IV ("the Fair") confiscated the man's home on the Rue des Jardins.

The House of Miracles: By royal decree and with papal backing, the desecrator's house was transformed into a chapel, known as the Chapel of the Miracles (later known as the Chapel des Billettes). The street was subsequently renamed Rue du Dieu bouilli (Street of the Boiled God).

Legacy and Relics

The miraculous Host was safely preserved and honored for centuries. However, the physical relic was permanently lost during the French Revolution in the late 18th century. Today, the story is formally documented by the Catholic Church as one of its recognized Eucharistic Miracles, and it is chronicled by historians like Giovanni Villani in the Miracoli Eucaristici Database.
