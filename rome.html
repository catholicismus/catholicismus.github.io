The Reported Event (June 11, 2000)

During the Feast of Pentecost in the Jubilee Year 2000, claims emerged from the Church of the "Mother of the Eucharist" in Rome. It was reported that during Holy Mass, led by Claudio Gatti, a consecrated Host began to emit real blood immediately following the consecration, which pooled upon the Host.

Historical and Canonical Context

This event holds a complex place in recent ecclesiastical history. While proponents and followers of the community surrounding the "Mother of the Eucharist" movement strongly believe in the miraculous nature of these occurrences, the Catholic Church has exercised caution.

  • Official Status: The claims associated with these events are not officially recognized by the Vatican.
  • Theological Discernment: The Church emphasizes that while God *can* work miracles, claims of this nature must undergo rigorous canonical scrutiny. Concerns regarding the clerical status of the celebrant involved have been a primary factor in the Church's reserved position.

Significance in Documentation

In our compendium of 3,000 miracles, it is vital to record not only the widely accepted miracles but also the events that have sparked significant public discussion. Documenting this event alongside its ecclesiastical status serves as an exercise in "discernment," reminding us that the Eucharist remains the central mystery of faith, regardless of specific claims.

"True faith seeks the truth, and truth requires both devotion and obedience to the Church's discernment."
