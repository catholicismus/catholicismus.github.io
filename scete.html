Scete, 3rd - 5th Century

← Back to Miracles

The Eucharistic Miracle of Scete (modern-day Wadi El Natrun, Egypt) occurred between the 3rd and 5th centuries. It addressed a monk who doubted the Real Presence. During Mass, the doubting monk saw the consecrated Host transform into a Child. An angel appeared, pierced the Child, and blood flowed into the chalice.

Key Details

  • Location: The desert of Scete (Wadi El Natrun), Egypt.
  • Date: 3rd to 5th Century.
  • The Doubt: A monk openly expressed his belief that the Eucharist was merely a symbol, rather than the literal Body and Blood of Christ.
  • The Intercession: Two older monks prayed that God would reveal the truth to their doubting brother.

The Event

The Vision: On a Sunday, during the consecration, the doubting monk and his companions no longer saw bread, but instead saw a living young child.

The Sign: When the priest broke the bread, an angel appeared with a sword and pierced the Child. The Child's blood poured directly into the chalice.

The Conclusion: When it was time to receive Communion, the monk was handed a piece of bleeding flesh. Terrified, he immediately professed his faith, and the flesh reverted to its traditional appearance.

Historical Recognition

This historical account is among the dozens of recognized Eucharistic events documented by the Vatican-approved Miracles of the Eucharist digital exhibition, which was initiated by the young patron saint of the internet, Saint Carlo Acutis.
