Wilsnack, 1383

← Back to Miracles

The 1383 Eucharistic Miracle of Wilsnack, Germany, refers to three Communion hosts discovered perfectly intact and bleeding after a fire destroyed the local church. This event drew massive medieval pilgrimages, though the bleeding was ultimately attributed to natural fungal growth and the relics were destroyed in 1558.

History of the Event

  • The Fire: In August 1383, the town of Bad Wilsnack was sacked and burned to the ground by Count Heinrich von Bülow.
  • The Discovery: Amid the charred ruins of the parish church, three completely intact Hosts were found bleeding continuously.
  • Confirmation: The local Bishop of Havelberg confirmed the "bleeding" in 1384, and Pope Urban VI contributed to the rebuilding of the church.

Impact and Decline

Pilgrimage Boom: The phenomenon transformed Bad Wilsnack into one of the most important pilgrimage sites in Northern Europe, second only to Rome and Santiago de Compostela.

Investigation: The bleeding was later widely attributed to Serratia marcescens, a natural, red-pigmented bacteria that can grow on bread and cause a blood-red appearance.

Destruction: During the Protestant Reformation, the veneration was condemned by reformers like Martin Luther, and the miraculous hosts were deliberately burned in 1558.
