Middleburg-Lovanio, 1374

← Back to Miracles

The Eucharistic Miracle of Middleburg-Lovanio (1374) is a famous historical account where a consecrated Host transformed into bleeding Flesh upon the tongue of a communicant. This remarkable event led to the division of the relic between the town of Middelburg (now in the Netherlands) and Louvain (Lovanio) in Belgium.

📜 The Story of the Miracle

  • The Devout Household: In 1374, a noblewoman in Middleburg, known for her deep faith, required her household staff to prepare for Easter through Lenten penance and confession.
  • The Sacrilegious Communion: A newly hired servant named Jan, who had neglected to go to confession, went up to receive Holy Communion at St. Peter's Church despite living in serious sin.
  • The Transformation: The moment the priest placed the Host on Jan's tongue, it instantly changed into bleeding human flesh.
  • The Flow of Blood: Terrified, Jan pulled the Host from his mouth. Drops of blood dripped onto the cloth covering the altar rail.
  • The Repentance: The parish priest carefully placed the miraculous Particle inside a vessel in the tabernacle, while Jan openly confessed his sin and amended his life.

🏛️ Splitting of the Relic

The miracle is deeply documented, most notably in a 1905 historical study by a professor at the Catholic University of Louvain. Because of its immense spiritual significance, the relic was divided:

  • Middelburg (St. Peter's Church): Retained a portion of the blood-stained Host and the altar cloths.
  • Louvain (Lovanio): The confessor of the local Archbishop requested a portion of the relic as a gift. It was given to the Augustinian Fathers and later moved to the Church of St. James (Sint-Jacobskerk) in Leuven.
