Dijon, 1430

← Back to Miracles

The 1430 Eucharistic Miracle of Dijon occurred when a woman in Monaco used a knife to remove a consecrated Host from a stolen monstrance. The Host miraculously bled, and as the blood dried, it imprinted an image of Christ seated on a throne, surrounded by instruments of the Passion.

Key Details and Timeline

  • The Incident: Unaware of Catholic teachings, the woman bought a monstrance from a second-hand dealer and tried to pry the Host out with a blade. When cut, the Host began dripping living blood that dried instantly.
  • The Image: The dried blood left a distinct imprint of Jesus sitting on a semicircular throne with instruments of the Passion beside him.
  • Transfer to Dijon: Horrified by what happened, the woman took the Host to a priest. Pope Eugene IV learned of the event and gave the relic to Duke Philip of Burgundy, who later donated it to the city of Dijon.
  • Destruction: The miraculous Host remained intact and was venerated for more than 350 years, primarily in the Basilica of Saint-Michel. On February 9, 1794, during the French Revolution, anti-clerical authorities burned the Host and converted the basilica into a Temple of Reason.

Where to Experience the History

Dijon Cathedral: Although the physical Host was destroyed, the miracle's legacy lives on. You can still see the main scene of the event depicted in the colorful stained-glass windows of the Cathedral of Saint-Benigne of Dijon.

Saint-Michel Basilica: At the Basilica of Saint-Michel, a special star marks the exact spot where the Host was burned in 1794. A Mass of Reparation is celebrated there annually to commemorate the event.

The Vatican: Historical documents and early artwork detailing the miracle are carefully cataloged in the Vatican's International Exhibition of Eucharistic Miracles.
