Kranenburg, 1280

← Back to Miracles

The 1280 Eucharistic miracle of Kranenburg (Germany) centers on a consecrated Host and a shepherd who, unable to swallow the Eucharist due to illness, threw it against a tree. When the tree was cut down years later, a perfectly carved crucifix miraculously appeared inside, linking the event to the sacred Host.

Historical Account

  • The Incident: In 1280, a local shepherd received Holy Communion but could not swallow the Host because of an illness. In a panic, he threw the Host into a hollow in a nearby tree.
  • Discovery: Plagued by remorse, he confessed his actions. Years later, when the shepherd decided to cut down the tree, the trunk split open, and a perfectly carved, life-sized crucifix fell to the ground.
  • Veneration: News of the crucifix—believed to have grown directly from the sacred Host—spread rapidly throughout the Lower Rhine region.

Legacy and Location

Church of St. Peter and Paul: To honor the miracle, the citizens of Kranenburg began constructing a church in 1408. Completed in 1444, it remains a major example of Gothic architecture and houses the Miraculous Crucifix.

Pilgrimage Site: Popes and bishops historically promoted devotion to the site. Pilgrims still visit the shrine in Kranenburg, near Kleve, Germany, to pray before the crucifix.
