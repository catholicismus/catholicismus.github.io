Rome, 595 AD

← Back to Miracles

The 6th–7th Century Eucharistic Miracle of Rome occurred in 595 AD and was directly witnessed by Pope St. Gregory the Great. This event, often known as the "Mass of St. Gregory," is one of the earliest documented Eucharistic miracles in Christian tradition.

The Event

While Pope St. Gregory was distributing Holy Communion, he noticed a noblewoman laughing during the liturgy. She confessed to him that she doubted the bread she herself had baked could truly transform into the Body and Blood of Christ. St. Gregory prayed for her, and before the entire congregation, the Host visibly transformed into real, bleeding flesh.

Historical Documentation

The primary historical source for this miracle is the Vita Beati Gregorii Papae (The Life of Blessed Pope Gregory), written in 787 AD by Paul the Deacon.

Relics and Legacy

  • Andechs Monastery (Germany): A portion of the physical relic is preserved and venerated here inside a precious monstrance.
  • San Gregorio Magno al Celio (Rome): This church houses a famous fresco by Nicolò Circignani that depicts this exact miraculous event.
