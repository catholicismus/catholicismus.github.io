La Rochelle, 1461

← Back to Miracles

The Eucharistic Miracle of La Rochelle (1461) refers to the instantaneous healing of a paralyzed and mute 12-year-old boy named Bertrand Leclerc on Easter Sunday. After receiving Holy Communion, his voice and mobility were fully restored, an event documented in a painted manuscript still preserved in the Cathedral of La Rochelle.

The Story Behind the Miracle

  • The Background: During Easter of 1461, a woman named Jehan Leclerc brought her 12-year-old son, Bertrand, to the Church of St. Bartholomew in La Rochelle, France. Due to a tragic accident when he was just seven, Bertrand had been entirely paralyzed and unable to speak.
  • The Communion: When it was time for Holy Communion, the boy intensely motioned to his mother that he wanted to receive the Eucharist. Initially, the priest refused, as the boy could not speak to make a confession. However, seeing the boy's desperate pleading, the priest allowed him to receive it.
  • The Healing: The moment Bertrand received the Host, he felt a jolt of mysterious force. He was instantly healed of his paralysis and regained his ability to speak. His very first words were, "Adjutorium nostrum in nomine Domini!" (Our help is in the name of the Lord!).

Historical Documentation

The Manuscript: The event is officially recognized by the local Church. The most authoritative historical document chronicling this miracle is a hand-written, illustrated manuscript from the era.

Where to See It: The original painted manuscript describing the miracle is still preserved today at the Cathedral of La Rochelle.
